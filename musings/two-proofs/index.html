Two Proofs

Suppose you are playing soccer with your friends, and you want to keep track of which players are the best team players in the offense. So whenever a goal is scored, you write down who the last two players to touch the ball in the scoring team were. You figure that if someone is a good team player in the offense, they will show up in many such pairs. Specifically. a good team player should play well with a variety of different people, and so you are not too interested if the same pair scores one, or two, or three goals in a game. You are only really interested in how many different people a given person is able to score goals with. Moreover, if someone scores a goal completely alone, then that doesn't count - you're interested in assessing team-playing capabilities, after all.

After keeping this score for a while, and ranking players by how many different people they have scored goals with, you begin to notice that you always seem to have ties somewhere in the ranking. This is obvious when there is only one goal in the game: each of the two scoring players will have scored a goal with one other person (that is, each other). But this shows up in more complicated patterns too: if Annie scores a goal with Benjamin, but Benjamin also scores a goal with Charlie, then Annie and Charlie are tied in second place, each with a single goal. Now you're curious, and you start imagining different game results, each more complicated than the other, trying to get a ranking of team players with no ties. After a while with no success, you start wondering whether this is impossible. It certainly seems so, but why would that be?

Eventually, you realize something interesting. Since there are eleven players to each team, each player can only score goals with at most ten other players. Additionally, of course, the minimum number of players someone could score goals with is zero. Between zero and ten, there are exactly eleven possible "scores" a player can get in the team-playing ranking, and you have exactly eleven players. If you want there to be no ties, you would need each of these eleven possible scores to be actual scores by players. If no player has a team-playing score of three, for example, then this means that two players must be tied with some other score, since there will only be ten possible different scores, for eleven players.

So now you know what scenario you need to draw up, and you get to work. But you still can't get all players to have distinct scores, and soon you realize why: a player can only have a score of zero if they score no goals with any other player. But a player can only have a score of ten if they score goals with all other players. You can't have both of these happen in the same match! If someone scored no goals with anyone else, then that means the maximum score someone else could have gotten is nine, not ten! Or alternatively, if Annie scored goals with everyone else in the team, then everyone else must have scored at least one goal with someone else - Annie, in the very least - and so no one can have a score of zero! Either way, the total number of possible scores is ten, not eleven. And since you have eleven players, that means there must be some single score which at least two players achieved.
